Modular [Laser Cut] Camera

Modular [Laser Cut] Camera was designed, built, and tested by Chris Curro and I in 24 hours at a Cooper Union Hackathon. As photoenthusiasts we decided to create a laser-cut modular camera system during the event. The camera has three modules. The first holds the lens(es), shutter and aperture. The second is a translatable focusing module. The third mounts a film or digital back. The configuration shown below holds a 120mm FL positive meniscus lens, a f/11 circular aperture, and a single blade leaf shutter. As a modular system, we created many other aperture shapes and sizes, and one could create different shutter mechanisms, optical lens stacks, and a variety of camera backs (digital, medium format, polaroid, etc.). My Nikon d90 was used during the Hackathon to efficiently demonstrate the potential of the Hack. Modular Camera won Most Technical Hack at HackCooper 2014.


An overview of the camera: On top you can see the circuit (arduino, op-amp, dip-switch, and push-button) that controls the shutter mechanism. Farthest to the right is the lens-aperture-shutter module. In the middle is the focusing module (this module is stationary and the first module slides in-and-out to focus). The largest wooden rectangular prism (on the left) is the film/digital-back module, which in this instance connects to my Nikon d90.


The setup of the lens-aperture-shutter module includes two laser-cut spacers to distance our lens from the other two mechanisms but also to demonstrate the potential variability of the system when given the extra space. For example, one could build a two-element lens system and still have space to insert all of the other components into the module.

The key systems assembled. The fabric on the back of the module allows for the smooth linear motion necessary for proper focusing.


A close up of hte shutter mechanism, showing each key component. A solenoid pin, when triggered, fires the bristol leaf-blade downward and a torsion spring returns the blade to its original position.
